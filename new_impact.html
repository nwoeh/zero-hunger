The Impact

Hunger doesn't just hurt. It changes who you become.

The effects of food insecurity go far beyond an empty stomach — they reshape children's brains, behavior, and future prospects.

1 in 3

People globally suffer from malnutrition

2×

More likely to have hyperactivity and attention issues in school

Lifelong

Higher rates of depression and chronic illness traced back to childhood hunger

In the classroom

A hungry child can't learn the same way.

Iron deficiency — one of the most common effects of food insecurity — directly impairs language development and the ability to follow directions. Hungry children are twice as likely to show hyperactivity and inattention in school, making it harder to keep up and stay engaged.

These aren't just short-term setbacks. Falling behind early creates gaps that compound over years of schooling, limiting opportunities long after the immediate hunger is gone.

Children who lack proper nutrition are twice as likely to show symptoms of hyperactivity and inattention as those with regular access to food.

The long game

The damage follows children into adulthood.

Childhood hunger is linked to higher rates of depression and suicidal ideation in adolescence. Repeated food insecurity acts as a form of chronic stress, increasing the likelihood of developing conditions like asthma that persist into adulthood.

Stunting and wasting — physical signs of prolonged undernutrition — affect brain development in ways that can't be fully reversed. These are permanent consequences of a preventable problem.
