Open letter

A letter to the United Nations

Dear Members of the United Nations,

I'm sure you know the discomfort of having to skip a meal because of a busy schedule. Now, think of the over 2.3 billion people across the globe who constantly live with this discomfort, over 800 million of whom were severely food insecure, meaning they had run out of food at times during the year and, at worst, gone an entire day or more without eating. In 2015, the United Nations committed to ending hunger by 2030 through Sustainable Development Goal 2. Over a decade later, the number of undernourished people worldwide has risen by over 150 million. The current approach has not delivered the necessary results, and this has to change.

It is imperative to recognize the extent of hunger's harmful effects on children, specifically how it affects their physical development, brain function, and mental health. Globally, one in three people suffer from malnutrition and children are the main victims of undernutrition which includes stunting, wasting, deficiencies of vitamins A and zinc. One of the most harmful aspects of food insecurity is iron deficiency, which has been linked to poor language comprehension and failure to follow directions. In schools, children who lack proper nutrition are twice as likely to show symptoms of hyperactivity and inattention as those who have proper access to food. The long-term effects are just as problematic, with data showing that childhood hunger is related to a higher risk of depression and suicidal ideation in adolescents. On top of this, hunger can be a form of toxic stress when experienced often, which can increase the likelihood of developing chronic conditions such as asthma. Hunger has many harmful effects on children and their development, and yet current efforts are failing to address the root causes of this crisis.

According to a survey of my peers, 100% of them believed that political interests were the biggest factor preventing us from solving hunger, and they're right. This political failure manifests in several different ways, starting with how we treat the food we already produce. Studies continue to show that global hunger is not caused by a lack of food, in fact, several estimates indicate that enough food is produced today to feed from 9 billion to almost 10 billion people. So why do we still have hungry people if we generate more than enough food? One of the biggest reasons lies in waste. While we produce plenty, the total estimate of food losses and waste is about one-third of edible food produced, or 1.3 billion tonnes per year. In high-income nations, food is discarded primarily at the end of the supply chain due to strict cosmetic standards that reject "ugly" produce, overproduction at the retail level, and wasteful consumer behavior. In contrast, in low-income countries, food is lost to inadequate infrastructure, specifically poor storage facilities that leave supplies vulnerable to pests and weather, a lack of refrigeration and insufficient transportation systems that fail to move crops from farms to markets. The problem is not that we lack food; it's that the food we produce never reaches the people who need it most.

On top of this, projections show that your progress toward the SDG2 goal has stalled. Currently we are on track to still have 582 million hungry people in 2030, far from the goal of zero hunger. So why aren't we on track? The answer lies in political priorities and a failure of institutional accountability. The barrier is not financial; predictions show that to bring 500 million people out of hunger by 2030 it would cost roughly $27 billion annually. This is just 0.0018% of the total tax revenue generated from members of the UN. To put this in perspective, global military spending exceeds $2.7 trillion annually, meaning the cost to end hunger represents roughly 1% of what the world dedicates to defense. The resources exist. Member states have simply chosen to allocate them elsewhere.

Beyond misplaced priorities, Sustainable Development Goal 2 lacks enforcement mechanisms to hold countries accountable. There are no binding commitments, no penalties for failing to contribute, and no coordinated global funding strategy. Rather than a unified strategy, hunger relief depends on fragmented efforts from countless organizations running small, disconnected programs that fail to address the crisis at the scale required. Member states have the capacity and the obligation to act. What is missing is not resources — it is the political commitment and mutual accountability needed to turn promises into action. These issues can be solved, and these goals can be reached. As the United Nations you are the pioneering force that can solve this issue so I ask that you take action.

First, you can push for policy changes to fight food waste, like having member states implement mandatory donation laws for large-scale retailers, following France, which became the first country in the world to ban supermarkets from destroying unsold food, forcing them to donate to charities instead. In addition to enforcing accountability through legal mandates, you must also provide clear rewards for sustainable action. Specifically, you should advocate for fiscal incentives and tax exemptions for businesses that redistribute surplus food, such as the "Last Minute Market" model in Italy, which grants retailers a discount on municipal waste taxes proportional to the quantity of food they donate. Furthermore, the global community needs a mandated harmonization of date labeling to end the confusing "best before" and "use by" stamps that currently drive consumers to throw away billions of pounds of safe, edible food.

While these solutions would address waste in high-income nations, low-income countries face different challenges, mainly food loss due to inadequate infrastructure. To address this you can press member states to provide financial assistance to smallholder farmers to build local warehouses and storage facilities that protect grain and produce from pests and weather, which currently cause losses of up to 60% after harvest. Furthermore, the development of rural road and rail networks is cited as a high-impact intervention to move crops quickly to market before they spoil. Addressing both waste in wealthy nations and loss in poor ones is critical to ensuring the food we produce actually reaches those who need it.

These infrastructure investments and policy changes would require funding, so I urge you to push for tax reforms to fund these goals. As stated previously, $27 billion annually is enough to take 500 million people out of hunger by 2030. This figure represents just 0.0018% of the total tax revenue generated from members of the UN. Increasing taxes by that much globally would mean paying an extra 18 cents for every $10,000 made per person, or the same as everyone in the world paying a little more than $3 each year. This $27 billion could also be generated by making the world's 58 million millionaires pay an extra $465 a year.

Beyond policies and funding, the final piece of the puzzle is to establish enforceable accountability mechanisms for SDG 2. This could include tying trade agreements or development aid to measurable progress on hunger reduction, creating public ranking systems that hold member states accountable for their contributions, or establishing a coordinated global hunger fund that pools resources and ensures strategic deployment. Binding commitments and transparent reporting are essential to turning the 2030 goal into reality rather than another broken promise.

This issue is urgent and we have to act now. There are over 800 million hungry people across the globe and this number is growing. You set a goal in 2015 to eradicate hunger by 2030 and it's not too late to still achieve this. The barrier here isn't resources or cost, we waste over 1.3 billion tonnes of food every year, and estimates show that just $27 billion annually could bring over 500 million people out of hunger by 2030. Solutions exist, like pushing for tax increases or implementing donation laws. As the United Nations you have the power to coordinate this effort and now I'm asking you to deliver on it.

Sincerely,
Noah Lefebvre
