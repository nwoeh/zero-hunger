The Problem

We already produce enough food for everyone.

Global hunger isn't caused by a lack of food. It's caused by waste, inequality, and political inaction.

Figure 1. Global food production far exceeds need, but waste and distribution failures leave hundreds of millions hungry.

Several estimates indicate that enough food is produced today to feed 9 to nearly 10 billion people — more than the entire current population of Earth. So why does hunger persist?

The answer lies in what happens between farm and table. Roughly one-third of all edible food produced globally — 1.3 billion tonnes per year — is lost or wasted before reaching a hungry person.

"The problem is not that we lack food; it's that the food we produce never reaches the people who need it most."

Where food is lost

The waste problem looks different in rich and poor nations.

High-income nations

Food is discarded at the end of the supply chain — strict cosmetic standards reject "ugly" produce, retailers overproduce, and consumers throw away food that's still safe to eat.

Low-income nations

Food is lost early due to inadequate storage, lack of refrigeration, and insufficient transportation that fails to move crops from farms to markets before they spoil — losses reach up to 60%.

Follow the money

The resources exist. The political will does not.

Ending hunger would cost roughly 1% of global military spending — or just 0.0018% of total UN member state tax revenue.

SDG 2 — the UN's commitment to Zero Hunger by 2030 — was made in 2015. Since then, the number of undernourished people has risen by over 150 million. We are on track to still have 582 million hungry people in 2030. Progress hasn't just stalled — it has reversed.
