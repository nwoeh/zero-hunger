The Crisis

We produce enough food for everyone. We just waste it.

Global hunger is not a supply problem. It's a distribution and political problem — and it's getting worse, not better.

The numbers

More than enough — yet hundreds of millions go hungry.

The world already produces enough food to feed up to 10 billion people. Yet over 800 million people are severely food insecure, and that number has been rising for years. Since 2015, the number of undernourished people worldwide has grown by over 150 million.

About one-third of all food produced globally — 1.3 billion tonnes — is lost or wasted every single year.

That wasted food could feed the world's hungry many times over. The problem isn't production. It's what happens between farm and table.

Figure 1. Global food production far exceeds need — the gap is caused by waste and unequal distribution, not scarcity.

Where food is lost

Waste looks different depending on where you live.

In wealthy countries, food is mostly wasted at the end of the chain — by retailers and consumers. In poorer countries, food is lost much earlier, before it even reaches a store.

In wealthy nations

Supermarkets reject "ugly" produce, overstock shelves, and discard unsold food. Consumers throw away food that's still perfectly safe to eat — often because of confusing "best before" labels.

In low-income nations

Food rots in fields or storage due to a lack of refrigeration, pest-proof warehouses, and roads to get crops to market quickly. Losses can reach 60% after harvest.

The politics

This is a solvable problem. We're just not solving it.

In 2015, the UN committed to ending hunger by 2030. We are not on track. At the current pace, 582 million people will still be hungry in 2030 — nearly the same as today.

Ending hunger vs. global military spending

Military spending

Ending hunger would cost about 1% of what the world spends on its military every year.

The money exists. What's missing is the political will to spend it — and pressure from ordinary people to demand change.
