Impact on Children

Hunger shapes children for life.

The consequences aren't just physical — they are cognitive, behavioral, and psychological, persisting long after childhood.

Cognitive effects

Iron deficiency undermines learning.

One of the most widespread effects of food insecurity is iron deficiency, directly linked to poor language comprehension and difficulty following directions. In schools, hungry children are twice as likely to display hyperactivity and inattention compared to well-fed peers.

Figure 2. Children lacking proper nutrition show double the rate of hyperactivity and inattention symptoms compared to well-fed peers (Ke & Ford-Jones, 2015).

Long-term effects

The damage extends far beyond the dinner table.

Childhood hunger is associated with a higher risk of depression and suicidal ideation in adolescence — a consequence that reaches far beyond physical health.

Repeated hunger also functions as a form of toxic stress, increasing the likelihood of chronic conditions such as asthma. Stunting, wasting, and micronutrient deficiencies compound over time — representing millions of children whose potential is being permanently diminished by a preventable crisis.
